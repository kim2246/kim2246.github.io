Companion Robot Interfaces for Older Adults

Experimental study on type, weight, and material preferences for elderly-friendly companion robots

Venue: AHFE International Conference on Human Factors in Robots and Unmanned Systems (2019; Springer proceedings 2020)
Status: Published
Authors: SooYeon Kim (lead), Young Hoon Oh, and Da Young Ju

Overview

This project investigated design elements that shape elderly users’ acceptance of companion robots, focusing on the physical attributes—type, weight, and material. Unlike earlier large-scale concept design work (2018, N=244), which explored animal vs. humanoid forms and functional themes, my study moved into controlled experimental testing with older adults to understand which tactile and visual features actually matter in practice.

Findings revealed that preferences varied by gender, living arrangement, and age group:

  • Women strongly disliked robots heavier than 2 kg.
  • Older adults living alone preferred synthetic fur textures, while those living with others rejected them due to hygiene concerns.
  • Across groups, microfiber was consistently the most preferred material, while plastic was least favored.

My Role

  • Project Lead: Conceived and designed the experimental protocol.
  • Developed stimuli prototypes (five robot types, six weight levels, four material panels).
  • Conducted data collection & analysis with elderly participants (SPSS, nonparametric tests).
  • Synthesized design implications into actionable guidelines for HRI designers.
  • First author of the AHFE publication.

Methods

  • Participants: 15 older adults (mean age ≈ 58; 80% female; 27% living alone).
  • Stimuli:
    • Robot types: bear, seal, dog, humanoid, newborn baby.
    • Weights: 150 g → 2.65 kg (six increments).
    • Materials: silicone, microfiber, synthetic fiber, plastic.
  • Procedure: Hands-on interaction + Likert-scale preference ratings.
  • Analysis: Nonparametric Mann–Whitney tests across gender, living arrangement, and age groups.

Outcomes

  • Material Matters: Microfiber ranked highest; plastic lowest.
  • Weight Sensitivity: Females showed a sharp decline in acceptance when weight > 2 kg.
  • Contextual Differences: Older adults living alone showed higher preference for synthetic fur, possibly due to its comforting familiarity.
  • Actionable Insights: Companion robots for older adults should be lightweight (<2 kg), soft-textured (microfiber), and animal-inspired in type to maximize acceptance.

Background


Image Source: Aging of South Korea in 2020 (Kee Whan Kim et al.)

With South Korea transitioning into an aged society faster than any OECD nation, addressing loneliness and depression in older adults is a pressing social issue. Companion robots are seen as potential solutions, offering emotional comfort and daily assistance without the challenges of pet ownership.

The 2018 living-lab study (N=244) identified that older adults prefer animal-like robots (bear, dog, seal) over humanoid forms, largely to avoid the uncanny valley effect. Building on that foundation, this AHFE project advanced to controlled testing of tactile and material preferences to inform real-world design specifications.
