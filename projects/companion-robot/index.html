Companion Robot Interfaces for Older Adults

Experimental study on type, weight, and material preferences for elderly-friendly companion robots

Venue: AHFE International Conference on Human Factors in Robots and Unmanned Systems (2019; Springer proceedings 2020)
Status: Published
Authors: SooYeon Kim , Young Hoon Oh, and Da Young Ju

Overview

This project investigated which physical design attributes—type, weight, and material—older adults prefer in companion robots.
Building on the 2018 large-scale concept design study (N=244), which identified animal-like robots as preferred and highlighted key functional needs, this study advanced into controlled experimental testing with older adults.

Key Findings

  • Weight: Women strongly disliked robots heavier than 2 kg.
  • Material: Microfiber was most preferred, plastic least.
  • Context: Living alone vs. with others influenced synthetic fur preference (comfort vs. hygiene).

My Role

  • Conceived and led the experimental study.
  • Designed prototypes for type, weight, and material testing.
  • Conducted data collection with older adult participants.
  • Statistically Analyzed results (Mann–Whitney U and Kruskal–Wallis tests).
  • Synthesized design guidelines

Methods

  • Participants: 15 older adults (mean age ≈ 58; 80% female; 27% living alone).
  • Stimuli:
    • Types: bear, seal, dog, humanoid, newborn baby.
    • Weights: 150 g → 2.65 kg across six increments.
    • Materials: silicone, microfiber, synthetic fiber, plastic.
  • Procedure: Hands-on evaluation + 5-point Likert ratings.
  • Analysis: Nonparametric tests by gender, age, living arrangement.

Background


Image Source: Aging of South Korea in 2020 (Kee Whan Kim et al.)

South Korea became an aged society in 2017, with >14% of its citizens over 65. Older adults face higher risks of isolation and depression. Companion robots are positioned as technological interventions that provide assistance, safety, and companionship.

Prior Concept Design Study (2018)

A large-scale survey & living-lab experiment (N=244) explored older adults’ design preferences:

  • Animal-like characters (bear, dog, seal) preferred over humanoids → reduced uncanny valley effect.
  • Desired qualities: warmth, familiarity, friendliness.
  • Functions clustered into safety & independence, health & care, social & daily life, and affective rapport.


These findings informed the variables tested in 2019 (type, weight, material).

Concept Functions

  • Safety & independence: misplaced item search, hazard alerts, emergency handling.
  • Health & care: medication reminders, vital monitoring, telemedicine support.
  • Social & daily life: family calls, event reminders, photo display, exercise prompts.
  • Affective rapport: animal-like character, warm tone/voice, friendly presence.

Outcomes

  • Lightweight (<2 kg), soft-textured robots are most accepted.
  • Microfiber consistently outperformed silicone, synthetic fiber, and plastic.
  • Gender and living context strongly shape acceptance patterns.
  • Findings offer specific, actionable design guidelines for elderly-friendly companion robots.
