Overview

This project synthesizes evidence on how AI/automation affects road-user well-being in mixed (human–AV) traffic. We define well-being across psychological (stress, trust, comfort), physical (safety, fatigue), social (cooperation, inclusion), and economic/time-use dimensions, and derive human-centered design principles—with emphasis on prosocial automation and trust calibration for safer, calmer, and more equitable mobility.

Literature Review — What We Learned about Well-being in Hybrid Mobility Societies

Definition & Framework

We frame well-being in hybrid mobility (human + automated road users) across four interdependent dimensions—physical, psychological, social, and economic/time-use—and treat them as a connected system rather than siloed outcomes. Safety/efficiency gains alone are insufficient unless stress, cognitive load, trust, inclusion, and access are addressed together.

Social well-being ties to social connectedness and inclusion (e.g., whether road users feel respected/accepted in mixed traffic); economic well-being links to access and opportunity via reliable mobility, with disproportionate risks for vulnerable groups when access is limited.

Measurement Toolkit (used in the reviewed studies)

Validated instruments recur across the literature:

  • WHO-5 (recent 2-week mental well-being)
  • STS — Satisfaction with Travel (affective + cognitive appraisal of trips)
  • PANAS (positive/negative affect balance)
  • WEMWBS (hedonic + eudaimonic mental well-being) These tools cover general mental well-being, travel-specific satisfaction, and affective states that shift with automation and interaction quality.

Empirical Insights (What helps vs. what hurts)

  1. Safety & Predictability drive relief. People feel best when automated behavior is predictably safe (smooth decel/accel, early yielding). Unexpected behavior or rare failures spike acute stress. Vigilance support (monitoring + timely alerts) maintains an optimal engagement state.

  2. Clear communication restores pedestrian trust. In the absence of a driver’s cues (eye contact, gestures), eHMI (lights/text cues) and/or traffic signals increase crossing confidence—provided behavior is consistent. Consistency across vehicles can matter more than novel signals.

  3. Prosocial (cooperative) automation reduces conflict and effort. AVs that yield courteously, leave gaps, and negotiate fairly diffuse tension and lower driver frustration—improving moment-to-moment well-being.

  4. Norm mismatch can increase friction. Aggressive human drivers may react negatively to over-cautious AVs; unmanaged mismatch raises annoyance and social tension.

Mechanisms (Why it works)

  • Trust calibration is central: under-trust → chronic anxiety; over-trust → risky disengagement. Interfaces that nudge toward appropriate engagement (limits transparency + graded alerts) maximize comfort without eroding safety.
  • Social signaling (yield cues, speed trajectory) substitutes for lost driver eye contact and enables smoother human–AV negotiation at crossings and merges.

Design & Policy Implications (Directly actionable)

  • Make behavior legible and consistent: smoother profiles, early slowing for yielding, standardized patterns across fleets to reduce uncertainty.
  • Use eHMI judiciously to complement—not replace—predictable vehicle behavior for pedestrians.
  • Encode prosocial policies (courteous merging, fair right-of-way) in planning and control to dampen conflict and elevate social/affective experience.
  • Measure what matters alongside classic safety/throughput metrics (stress, comfort, travel satisfaction).
  • Educate users on capabilities and limits to avoid under-/over-trust.

Gaps & Open Questions

  • Social inclusion is under-measured: few studies quantify whether different groups (e.g., older adults, less tech-savvy users) feel traffic is “for them” in automated contexts.
  • Longitudinal evidence is thin: we need months-to-years studies on adaptation, over-trust, and health outcomes.
  • Vulnerable road users (pedestrians, cyclists) remain underrepresented relative to in-vehicle research.

Key Takeaways

  • Safety + predictability = lower stress and better physical/mental states, especially when vigilance is supported.
  • Communicative and prosocial automation (legible yielding, courteous merges, consistent norms) improves trust and everyday experience for drivers and pedestrians.
  • Use WHO-5 / STS / PANAS / WEMWBS to capture well-being, travel satisfaction, and affect shifts that automation can influence.

Selected Examples (Design Patterns)

  • Takeover UX: progressive alerts → brief rationale (“slowing for cut-in”) → countdown → confirmation.
  • eHMI for pedestrians: early deceleration + front-light “Yield” cue → higher crossing confidence.
  • Prosocial merge: AV leaves a cooperative gap and signals intent → lower human driver stress/aggression.
